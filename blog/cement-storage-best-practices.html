Cement Storage Best Practices

Don't let your cement go to waste! Learn how to store cement properly to maintain its quality and strength for months. Essential tips for contractors, homeowners, and builders in Kenya.

Cement is one of the most important materials in construction 50% of its strength in just a few weeks.

This guide covers everything you need to know about storing cement properly in Kenya. Whether you're a contractor storing 500 bags or a homeowner with just a few bags, these tips will help you protect your investment and build stronger structures.

Cement absorbs moisture from the air. Even a small amount of moisture can cause cement to hydrate prematurely, reducing its strength and making it unusable for construction.

Why Proper Cement Storage Matters

Maintains Strength

Properly stored cement retains its full strength. Poor storage can reduce strength by up to 50%.

Saves Money

Well-stored cement lasts longer, reducing waste and the need to buy replacement bags.

Ensures Quality

Consistent quality means stronger, more durable structures without unexpected failures.

Reduces Delays

Good storage means you always have usable cement when you need it, avoiding project delays.

The 5 Golden Rules of Cement Storage

1

Keep It Dry

Store cement in a dry, moisture-free area. Water is cement's worst enemy.

2

Raise Off the Ground

Store bags on pallets or raised platforms, at least 15-20cm above the floor.

3

Keep Away from Walls

Maintain a gap between bags and walls to prevent dampness from condensation.

4

Use Within 6 Months

Cement has a shelf life of approximately 6 months (unopened). Use it fresh.

5

Cover Properly

Protect bags with tarpaulin or plastic sheeting if storing outdoors.

Step-by-Step Cement Storage Guide

1. Choose the Right Storage Location

Storage Type Suitability Requirements
Indoor Storage Dry building, good ventilation, no leaks
Covered Outdoor Tarpaulin cover, raised platform, good drainage
Open Outdoor High risk of moisture damage
Under Construction Keep covered and off the ground

2. Prepare the Storage Area

  • Check for leaks: Ensure the roof doesn't leak and walls aren't damp.
  • Clean the floor: Remove any debris, oil, or moisture.
  • Use pallets: Place wooden pallets or raised platforms on the floor.
  • Ensure ventilation: Good airflow prevents moisture buildup.
  • Check drainage: Ensure water can't flow into the storage area.

In coastal areas like Mombasa, Kilifi, and Lamu, use extra moisture protection due to high humidity. Consider using dehumidifiers or storing cement in sealed containers if possible.

3. Stack Cement Properly

  • Maximum height: Stack no more than 10 bags high to prevent crushing and spoilage
  • Stagger stacks: Stack bags in a herringbone pattern or in rows with gaps for air circulation
  • Leave gaps: Leave small gaps between bags for air circulation
  • First in, first out (FIFO): Use the oldest cement first

4. Protect from Moisture

Use Tarpaulin

Cover stacks with waterproof tarpaulin, especially near doors or windows.

Keep Away from Walls

Maintain 30-50cm gap from walls to prevent condensation damage.

Raise Platforms

Use pallets or raised platforms at least 15-20cm above the floor.

Good Airflow

Ensure proper ventilation in the storage area to reduce humidity.

5. Monitor and Maintain

  • Regular inspections: Check for signs of moisture, lumps, or damage weekly.
  • Check for pests: Rodents and insects can damage bags and contaminate cement.
  • Re-stack if needed: Re-stack bags that have shifted or settled.
  • Keep records: Track purchase dates and use FIFO to avoid old stock.
  • Emergency preparation: Have a plan for sudden rains or leaks.

How to Check if Cement is Still Good

Test What to Look For Result
Visual Inspection Lumps, hard spots, discoloration Hard lumps = moisture damage
Feel Test Smooth, fine powder consistency Gritty or clumpy = poor quality
Water Test Mix with water and check setting time Slow or no setting = bad cement
Check Date Manufacturing date on bag Over 6 months = reduced strength
Float Test Drop cement in water Floats = good, sinks = poor

Take a handful of cement and squeeze it in your palm. Good quality cement should be smooth and fine and should flow like powder. If it's clumpy or gritty, it has been exposed to moisture and may not perform well.

Common Cement Storage Mistakes to Avoid

  • Storing on bare ground: Ground moisture will ruin cement quickly.
  • Stacking too high: Over 10 bags high can crush and damage bags.
  • No cover: Even indoors, cover stacks near doors or windows.
  • Ignoring leaks: Even small leaks can damage many bags.
  • Not using FIFO: Old cement loses strength over time.
  • Storing near chemicals: Certain chemicals can degrade cement.
  • Storing outside: Outdoor storage exposes cement to weather.

Special Considerations for Kenya

  • Coastal areas (Mombasa, Kilifi, Lamu): High humidity requires extra moisture protection. Use plastic covers and consider dehumidifiers.
  • Nairobi & Central Kenya: Moderate climate but still require dry storage. Rainy seasons need extra protection.
  • Rift Valley (Nakuru, Eldoret): Cool temperatures are good but watch for condensation.
  • Northern Kenya: Hot and dry
  • Rainy seasons: Always have emergency covers ready for sudden rains.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long can cement be stored?

Cement can be stored for up to 6 months if properly stored—dry, off the ground, and protected from moisture. However, using it within 3 months is recommended for maximum strength.

What happens if cement gets wet?

Cement that gets wet hydrates prematurely, forming hard lumps. It loses significant strength and may be unusable. In some cases, it can be used for non-critical applications, but it is not recommended for structural work.

How can I tell if cement is expired?

Check for hard lumps, gritty texture, or discolouration. If the cement feels clumpy or has visible lumps, it has absorbed moisture and should not be used for structural work. Always check the manufacturing date on the bag.

Can I store cement outside?

Outdoor storage is not recommended due to rain, humidity, and temperature changes. If you must store outside, use a raised platform, cover it with heavy tarpaulin, and use plastic sheeting underneath.

How should I store cement on a construction site?

On-site, store cement in a dry area off the ground. Use pallets, cover with tarpaulin, and maintain good airflow. Always use FIFO (First In, First Out) to ensure old stock is used first.

Conclusion

Proper cement storage is essential for building strong, durable structures. By following these best practices, you can:

  • Save money by reducing waste
  • Build stronger with full-strength cement
  • Reduce delays by always having usable cement on hand
  • Avoid frustration from spoilage and wasted materials

Remember: Keep it dry, raise it up, and use it fresh. Your cement is too valuable to waste!
